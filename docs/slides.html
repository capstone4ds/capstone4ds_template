Capstone Presentation

Namita Mishra

Autumn Wilcox

Published

October 24, 2025

Type 2 Diabetes (T2D), a public health concern worldwide

  • Body doesn’t make enough insulin or is not used properly → rise in blood sugar
  • Affects organs and can lead to multi-organ failure
  • Understanding risk factors (age, BMI, genetics) allows early intervention

Statistical Problem

  • Traditional statistical approaches fail to analyze complex relationships or uncertainty present in healthcare data
  • Missing data in survey-collected datasets reduces sample size and may bias estimates

Study Goal

  • Address statistical challenges where traditional methods fail
  • Compare Frequentist and Bayesian methods on NHANES 2013–2014
  • Identify associations between risk factors and diabetes (dichotomous outcome)
  • Demonstrate model performance and insights for healthcare data analysis

Methods

  • Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) of weighted NHANES dataset revealed missing values
  • Multivariate linear regression on complete cases resulted in reduced sample size
  • Multiple Imputation by Chained Equations (MICE) was applied to handle missing data
  • Bayesian regression was conducted on imputed data after normalizing age and BMI

Bayesian Regression Principles

  • Priors stabilize estimates and reduce extreme values
  • Handles uncertainty fully through posterior distributions
  • Incorporates prior knowledge for robust inference
  • Works effectively with imputed datasets

Data Source

  • NHANES 2013–2014 (CDC)
  • Files merged: demographics (DEMO_H), exam (BMX_H), questionnaire (DIQ_H)
  • Cleaned dataset: n = 10,175 observations, 10 variables
Dataset Key variables Dimensions
demo1 age, race, gender 10175 × 47
exam1 BMI 9813 × 224
quest1 Diabetes 9813 × 953

Data Exploration

  • Small sample for certain BMI subgroups (pre-diabetic n=132/9813)
  • Only 14 complete cases across all variables → imbalance
  • Survey-weighted proportions approximate US population (Male:Female ~48.9%:51%)
  • Predictor correlations are hierarchical and complex → suitable for Bayesian modeling

Our Question

  • Can Bayesian logistic regression provide more stable and transparent inference than classical MLE for diabetes outcomes in NHANES 2013–2014?
